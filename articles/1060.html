2) CRGP

C — Context

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are a key interpretability tool for LLMs, but evaluating SAE quality is challenging because true features are unknown. SAEBench is the standard evaluation suite, relying on proxy metrics like sparse probing, TPP, SCR, autointerp, and RAVEL. The field depends on these metrics to compare SAE architectures, but their reliability has not been systematically audited.

R — Related work

  • Bricken et al. (2023) introduced SAEs for LLM interpretability.
  • Marks et al. (2023) proposed TPP and SCR metrics for SAE evaluation.
  • Kantamneni et al. (2024) developed sae-probes, a variant of sparse probing with more datasets and cross-validated regularization.
  • Paulo et al. (2024) introduced autointerp for automated latent interpretation.
  • Huang et al. (2023) proposed RAVEL for evaluating disentanglement and causal effects.

G — Gap

Previous work assumed SAEBench metrics reliably rank SAEs, but no systematic audit existed. The field lacks understanding of metric noise, ground-truth correlation, and discriminability, leading to potential false progress in SAE architecture research.

P — Proposal

The author audits six SAEBench metrics through three lenses: reseed noise on a fixed SAE, ground-truth correlation on synthetic SAEs, and discriminability across training trajectories. Key insight: TPP and SCR fail multiple lenses at canonical settings, while sae-probes is the most reliable but still noisy for similar architectures.

4) Experiments

Main results

Reseed noise: sae-probes (k=5) CV=0.2%, TPP top-50 CV=23%, SCR top-500 CV=6.2%. Ground-truth correlation: TPP and SCR become negatively correlated with ground truth at large top-N. Discriminability: sae-probes separates cross-architecture SAEs but struggles with Matryoshka variants (same architecture, different hyperparameters).

Ablation

Increasing the number of probing datasets from 5 to 113 (sae-probes vs sparse probing) reduces reseed noise CV from 0.3% to 0.2% and improves discriminability. Cross-validated L1 regularization further stabilizes scores.

Limitations

['Only audits SAEBench metrics, not other evaluation approaches like supervised baselines.', 'Synthetic SAEs may not fully capture real LLM feature complexity.', 'Limited to Gemma-2-2b and a few other models; generalizability to larger or different architectures is untested.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Highlights that widely used metrics (TPP, SCR) can mislead SAE research, wasting effort on false improvements.
  2. Provides concrete noise thresholds (e.g., minimum reliable difference for sae-probes is 0.008) for practitioners to interpret single-seed results.
  3. Suggests that scaling dataset size and using cross-validated regularization (as in sae-probes) is a promising direction for more reliable benchmarks.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Replicate the audit on larger LLMs (e.g., 7B, 70B) to test generalizability.
  • [ ] Develop new SAE benchmarks with lower noise and higher discriminability, inspired by sae-probes design choices.
  • [ ] Investigate why TPP and SCR fail (e.g., probe overfitting, spurious correlations) and propose fixes or alternatives.
  • [ ] Integrate multi-seed evaluation as standard practice in SAE benchmarking.
