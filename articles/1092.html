2) CRGP

C — Context

Post-training for LLMs has split into two tracks: online RL with verifiable rewards (e.g., GRPO) excels at math/code but cannot handle open-ended tasks, while preference optimization (e.g., DPO, SimPO) handles open-ended generation but lacks continuous exploration. Scalar reward models used in online RL for open-ended tasks suffer from reward hacking because they collapse multi-dimensional quality into a single proxy.

R — Related work

  • GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) provides critic-free online RL with group-relative advantages, used with verifiable rewards for math/code.
  • DPO, SimPO, SPPO, and Nash-MD are offline/game-theoretic preference optimization methods that avoid explicit reward modeling but forgo online exploration.
  • RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards) uses programmatic verifiers for math/code but cannot extend to open-ended tasks.
  • Multi-objective RLHF returns a Pareto family from independent scalar rewards, requiring multiple training runs.
  • General Preference Model (GPM) embeds responses into k skew-symmetric subspaces to represent intransitive preferences and multi-dimensional quality.

G — Gap

Scalar reward models used in online RL for open-ended tasks are incomplete proxies for multi-dimensional quality, causing reward hacking as the policy exploits whichever axis the RM is most sensitive to. Existing preference optimization methods lack continuous exploration, while online RL with scalar RMs cannot maintain balanced improvement across quality dimensions.

P — Proposal

GPRL treats the General Preference Model (GPM) as a structured, multi-dimensional reward source for GRPO-style online RL. It computes per-dimension group-relative advantages across GPM's k subspaces, normalizes each on its own scale, and aggregates them with context-dependent eigenvalues, preventing any single axis from dominating. A closed-loop drift monitor detects and corrects single-axis exploitation by reweighting dimensions and tightening the KL trust region during training.

4) Experiments

Main results

Starting from Llama-3-8B-Instruct, GPRL achieves 56.51% length-controlled win rate on AlpacaEval 2.0, outperforming GRPO+BT by 14.59 points. It also beats SimPO and SPPO on Arena-Hard, MT-Bench, and WildBench, and resists reward hacking across extended training runs.

Ablation

Using k=3 subspaces (from Skywork-Reward) saturates performance; the drift monitor reweights dimensions when variance profile drifts, preventing single-axis exploitation without external evaluation signals.

Limitations

GPRL requires training a GPM embedding model, adding complexity compared to scalar RM approaches. The optimal number of subspaces k is dataset-dependent and may not generalize across domains. The drift monitor's threshold tuning may require task-specific calibration.

5) Why it matters

  1. Provides a principled way to combine online exploration with multi-dimensional preference signals, directly addressing reward hacking in open-ended tasks.
  2. The per-dimension advantage normalization and drift monitor offer a general framework for stable multi-objective online RL without separate reward models.
  3. Demonstrates that intransitivity-aware preference models can be effectively integrated into policy optimization, not just preference ranking.
  4. Achieves SOTA results on multiple benchmarks while maintaining robustness over extended training, relevant for production alignment pipelines.
