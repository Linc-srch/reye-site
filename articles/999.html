1) Why-read

2) CRGP

C — Context

Unified autoregressive models for image generation and text understanding suffer from modality competition during pretraining, where high-variance visual gradients dominate and suppress text signals. Existing solutions focus on architectural changes or external gradient balancing, but first-order optimizers like AdamW fail to capture cross-modal covariance. Second-order methods like K-FAC are memory-prohibitive for modern VLMs.

R — Related work

  • Native unified VLMs (Janus, Emu3) treat both modalities equally via next-token prediction but exacerbate modality competition.
  • Modality conflict mitigation via dynamic loss weighting, gradient projection, and curriculum scheduling (e.g., MGDA, GradNorm) operates externally to the optimizer.
  • Efficient second-order preconditioners: Shampoo (uncentered moments vulnerable to outliers), SOAP (robust eigenbasis normalization), and K-FAC (memory-heavy).
  • Fisher-Orthogonal Projection (FOP) accelerates multimodal convergence but relies on K-FAC.

G — Gap

First-order optimizers (AdamW) cannot handle cross-modality gradient heterogeneity, while existing second-order methods either are memory-prohibitive (K-FAC) or fail under large-batch training (Shampoo). Even SOAP leaves a lingering trade-off where text understanding is sacrificed for visual generation. No prior work addresses fine-grained variance correction across massive gradient accumulation windows.

P — Proposal

ML-FOP-SOAP combines SOAP preconditioning with a Fisher-Orthogonal Projection (FOP) that projects gradient differences orthogonally to the primary descent trajectory, neutralizing modality conflicts. A Multi-Level Hierarchical Gradient Folding mechanism (inspired by MLMC) recursively fuses cumulative snapshots across micro-steps to capture intra-window variance without per-step overhead, enabling stable training at batch size 8192.

4) Experiments

Main results

On Janus and Emu3 trained on LLaVA-3M/12M, ML-FOP-SOAP achieves 1.4x sample efficiency and 1.5x wall-clock speedup over AdamW. It maintains Pareto improvements in both visual generation and text understanding, with stable convergence at batch size 8192 where AdamW and Shampoo degrade catastrophically.

Ablation

Removing FOP (using SOAP alone) recovers the text-vision trade-off; removing multi-level folding (applying FOP only at accumulation boundaries) reduces gains by ~30% in sample efficiency. The dyadic telescoping strategy adds <5% overhead vs. per-step projection.

Limitations

['Method is validated only on Janus and Emu3; generalization to other multimodal architectures (e.g., diffusion-based) is untested.', 'Multi-level folding introduces hyperparameters (number of levels, folding frequency) that may require tuning per model scale.', 'SOAP preconditioner still requires storing left/right eigenvectors, which may become memory-intensive for extremely wide layers.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Directly addresses modality competition at the optimizer level, a root cause often overlooked in favor of architectural fixes.
  2. Enables ultra-large-batch training (8192) for multimodal models, crucial for scaling to web-scale data and reducing wall-clock time.
  3. The multi-level variance correction technique is a novel application of MLMC ideas to gradient accumulation, potentially transferable to other multi-task or multi-modal settings.
  4. Provides a practical second-order optimizer that is memory-efficient (via SOAP) and geometry-aware (via FOP), bridging theory and practice.
