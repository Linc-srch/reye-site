1) Why-read

IBAL proposes an information-theoretic adversarial attack that breaks inter-agent coordination by minimizing mutual information between groups, and trains robust policies under such disruptions.

2) CRGP

C — Context

Cooperative MARL under CTDE learns coordinated policies that are brittle under perturbations. Prior robust MARL methods focus on value-oriented attacks (e.g., minimizing Q-values) but do not explicitly model attacks that disrupt the interaction structure itself.

R — Related work

  • Robust MARL via regularization (Lin et al., 2020; Bukharin et al., 2023) and distributional RL (Li et al., 2020; Du et al., 2024).
  • Adversarial attacks in single-agent RL targeting states/observations (Zhang et al., 2020a), actions (Pinto et al., 2017), or rewards (Rakhsha et al., 2021).
  • Multi-agent adversarial attacks: EGA (Yuan et al., 2023) diversifies value-minimizing attacks; Wolfpack (Lee et al., 2025) sequentially targets agents.
  • Information-theoretic MARL uses mutual information for influence estimation (Jaques et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022) and structured exploration (Mahajan et al., 2019).

G — Gap

Existing robust MARL methods primarily consider value-oriented attacks (e.g., minimizing Q-values) or simple observation noise. They do not explicitly model attacks that break the inter-agent interaction structure, which can cause coordination to collapse abruptly even under mild perturbations.

P — Proposal

IBAL partitions agents into two groups and uses mutual information (MI) to quantify cross-group influence. The attacker occludes cross-group observations and perturbs actions to minimize MI, thereby breaking coordination. Agents are trained via a POMDP formulation that is equivalent to the induced Dec-POMDP under attack, enabling robust policy learning.

4) Experiments

Main results

IBAL outperforms baselines (QMIX, RQL, EGA, Wolfpack) on StarCraft II (SMAC) across diverse attack settings. Under agent-missing scenarios (e.g., 1 agent removed), IBAL achieves ~40% higher win rate than the best baseline. Under observation attacks, IBAL maintains ~85% win rate vs. ~60% for baselines.

Ablation

Ablation on the MI-based attack objective shows that using MI to select which observations to occlude is critical: random occlusion drops win rate by ~20% compared to MI-guided occlusion.

Limitations

['The method assumes a fixed partition of agents into two groups, which may not capture more complex interaction topologies.', 'The MI estimation relies on sampling and may be inaccurate in high-dimensional observation spaces.', 'Evaluation is limited to SMAC; generalization to other domains (e.g., multi-robot control) is not shown.']

5) Why it matters

  1. IBAL introduces a novel threat model (interaction-breaking) that is more realistic for multi-agent systems where coordination is key.
  2. The information-theoretic perspective provides a principled way to design attacks and defenses beyond value-based objectives.
  3. The method shows strong robustness even under agent dropout, which is relevant for real-world deployment where agents may fail.
  4. The POMDP equivalence proof provides theoretical grounding for adversarial training under interaction attacks.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Test IBAL on continuous control tasks (e.g., Multi-Agent MuJoCo) to assess scalability.
  • [ ] Extend the attack to dynamic group partitioning or hierarchical interaction structures.
  • [ ] Combine IBAL with communication-based MARL to study robustness under corrupted messages.
