WhoSaidIt: Human-LLM Collaborative Annotation for Text-Based Multilingual Speaker-Attribute Classification

WhoSaidIt：基于人机协作标注的多语言说话人属性分类

content-management editorial-workflow digital-publishing AI & Machine Learning

Lingyu Gao, Will Monroe, David Smith, Meghan Jemison, Jackie Lee

Summary

Key claim: A human-LLM collaborative re-annotation framework can stabilize noisy multilingual speaker-attribute labels; key observation: LLMs help surface cross-lingual rationales but remain sensitive to surface cues and struggle with subtle pragmatic distinctions.

摘要

本文提出了一种人类与LLM协作的重新标注框架，用于稳定主观的多语言说话人属性标签。通过构建WhoSaidIt数据集（覆盖11种语言、9种属性），揭示了跨语言标注决策的显著差异，并展示了LLM在辅助发现跨语言标注理由方面的潜力与局限。

2) CRGP

C — Context

Speaker-attribute classification from text is inherently ambiguous, especially in multilingual settings where demographic and social cues are implicit and culturally variable. Prior work focuses on monolingual or user-level profiling, but less attention has been paid to consistent cross-lingual annotation of short utterances. Existing noisy corpora from production workflows suffer from partial coverage and inconsistent label definitions.

R — Related work

  • Subjective multilingual annotation often produces systematic disagreement due to cultural and pragmatic differences (joshi-etal-2016-cultural, plank-2022-problem, sandri-etal-2023-dont).
  • LLMs have been used for active learning, verification-based correction, and interactive annotation (Kholodna et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; kim-etal-2024-meganno).
  • LLMs have been applied to improve entity-recognition guidelines (bibal-etal-2025-automating) and simulate pilot annotation (Kim and Yoon, 2026).
  • Author profiling and demographic prediction from text often use long user histories or metadata (Bamman et al., 2014; Guimarães et al., 2017; PAN shared tasks).

G — Gap

Existing annotation frameworks for speaker attributes either rely on acoustic signals or assume long user-level text with metadata, and do not address the challenge of consistent cross-lingual labeling from short, implicit utterances. Noisy production corpora lack systematic methods to refine label definitions and resolve annotator disagreement across languages under resource constraints.

P — Proposal

The authors propose a human-LLM collaborative re-annotation framework that uses LLMs to surface recurring cross-lingual annotation rationales from a noisy corpus, which human experts consolidate into unified guidelines. Disagreement-focused sampling targets high-information ambiguous instances for re-annotation, and refined rationales are incorporated into LLM prompts for iterative quality control.

---

4) Experiments

Main results

The WhoSaidIt dataset covers 11 languages and 9 binary attributes; a public subset of 3,600 examples across 5 languages is released. The framework quantifies divergence between original and revised annotations, benchmarks recent LLMs, and analyzes the effect of explicit rationales on model behavior. Results show substantial cross-lingual differences in annotation decisions.

Ablation

Disagreement-focused sampling efficiently prioritizes high-information ambiguous instances, enabling targeted re-annotation under resource constraints; the framework reduces noise compared to the original noisy corpus of ~195,000 examples.

Limitations

['LLMs remain sensitive to surface cues and struggle with subtle pragmatic distinctions across languages.', 'The framework relies on expert annotators for consolidation, which may still be resource-intensive for very large-scale or low-resource languages.', 'The public subset is limited to 5 languages and 3,600 examples, which may not capture full cross-lingual variability.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Directly addresses challenges in multilingual annotation for production systems where speaker attributes must be inferred from short text without user history.
  2. Provides a practical human-LLM collaboration template that balances automation with expert oversight, relevant for industrial content-labeling workflows.
  3. Highlights the importance of cross-lingual consistency in subjective annotation, which is critical for fair and robust multilingual NLP systems.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Apply the framework to additional languages and attributes to test scalability and generalizability.
  • [ ] Investigate methods to reduce LLM sensitivity to surface cues, e.g., through contrastive prompting or rationale-based training.
  • [ ] Explore automated consolidation of rationales to reduce expert workload while maintaining quality.
  • [ ] Evaluate the impact of the re-annotated dataset on downstream speaker-attribute classification model performance.

1) 阅读理由

核心主张：人类与LLM协作的重新标注框架能够稳定噪声多语言说话人属性标签；关键观察：LLM有助于发现跨语言标注理由，但对表面线索敏感且难以处理微妙的语用区别。

2) CRGP（背景 / 相关工作 / 差距 / 方案）

背景：从文本进行说话人属性分类本身具有歧义性，尤其是在多语言环境中，人口统计和社会线索往往是隐性的且因文化而异。先前工作主要关注单语言或用户级画像，但对短话语的一致跨语言标注关注不足。现有生产流程中的噪声语料库存在覆盖不全和标签定义不一致的问题。

相关工作：主观多语言标注常因文化和语用差异产生系统性分歧；LLM已被用于主动学习、基于验证的纠错和交互式标注；LLM还被用于改进实体识别指南和模拟试点标注；作者画像和人口统计预测通常使用长用户历史或元数据。

差距：现有的说话人属性标注框架要么依赖声学信号，要么假设有元数据的长用户文本，未能解决从短隐含话语进行一致跨语言标注的挑战。噪声生产语料库缺乏系统方法来细化标签定义并解决资源约束下跨语言的标注者分歧。

提议：作者提出一种人类与LLM协作的重新标注框架，利用LLM从噪声语料库中发现跨语言重复出现的标注理由，由人类专家整合为统一指南。基于分歧的采样针对高信息量的模糊实例进行重新标注，并将精炼后的理由纳入LLM提示以实现迭代质量控制。

3) 实验

WhoSaidIt数据集覆盖11种语言和9个二元属性；公开发布了一个包含5种语言、3600个样本的子集。该框架量化了原始标注与修订标注之间的差异，对近期LLM进行了基准测试，并分析了显式理由对模型行为的影响。结果显示跨语言标注决策存在显著差异。

消融实验

基于分歧的采样高效优先处理高信息量的模糊实例，使得在资源约束下能够进行针对性重新标注；与原始约19.5万样本的噪声语料库相比，该框架有效降低了噪声。

局限性

['LLM对表面线索敏感，难以处理跨语言的微妙语用区别。', '该框架依赖专家标注者进行整合，对于大规模或低资源语言可能仍然资源密集。', '公开子集仅限5种语言和3600个样本，可能无法捕捉完整的跨语言变异性。']

4) 重要性

  1. 直接解决了生产系统中需从短文本推断说话人属性而无用户历史的多语言标注挑战。
  2. 提供了一个实用的人机协作模板，平衡了自动化与专家监督，适用于工业内容标注流程。
  3. 强调了主观标注中跨语言一致性的重要性，这对公平且稳健的多语言NLP系统至关重要。
