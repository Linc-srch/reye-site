2) CRGP

C — Context

Generative AI is increasingly embedded in online platforms to polish user posts (LinkedIn) or provide context (X). Prior work has shown LLMs express biased opinions and can shape individual opinions during human-AI interactions, but the impact on collective opinion formation when AI mediates human-to-human communication has been underexplored.

R — Related work

  • LLMs carry biases in expressed opinions on politically salient topics (e.g., Hartmann et al., 2023)
  • LLMs can shift individuals' expressed opinions without awareness during assisted writing (e.g., Jakesch et al., 2023)
  • Mathematical sociology models of opinion dynamics under social influence (e.g., Friedkin & Johnsen, 1990)
  • Levers for influencing opinion formation via key individuals or network perturbations (e.g., Abebe et al., 2018)

G — Gap

Existing work focuses on AI shaping individual opinions in direct human-AI interactions, but neglects how AI-mediated communication (e.g., LLM editing posts) can influence collective opinion formation across a social network. There is no formal model capturing the amplification of AI biases through network dynamics.

P — Proposal

The paper combines empirical analysis of LLM biases in editing human-written texts on 13 contested topics with a mathematical model extending Friedkin-Johnsen opinion dynamics, where an AI system transforms opinions expressed and perceived between users. They analytically characterize equilibrium shifts and simulate on real social network data, showing that small average biases can be amplified into large collective opinion shifts.

4) Experiments

Main results

LLMs from four families (Llama-3.1-8B, Ministral-3-8B, gemma-3-12b-it, Qwen3-8B) introduced directional biases across topics; e.g., gemma-3-12b-it nudged texts in favor of gun control and against atheism. On real social network data (e.g., Twitter followers graph), simulations showed that a bias of 0.05 in individual opinion transformation led to a collective opinion shift of 0.15 at equilibrium. Audit of X's 'Explain this post' found Grok exhibited pro-life bias on abortion content, traced to a specific guideline.

Ablation

Varying the strength of AI bias (transformation parameter) from 0.01 to 0.1 showed that the equilibrium collective shift is approximately 3x the average per-interaction bias, with network structure (e.g., density, polarization) modulating amplification.

Limitations

['The empirical bias analysis uses synthetic LLM outputs rather than real user behavior on platforms.', 'The opinion dynamics model assumes a fixed AI transformation function, not accounting for adaptive or personalized AI systems.', 'The X audit is limited to one feature and one topic (abortion), reducing generalizability.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Directly relevant to your work on AI safety and system safety: shows how seemingly benign AI mediation can have systemic, network-level effects on opinion formation, a form of emergent risk.
  2. Connects to digital identity: AI-mediated communication alters how users express and perceive identities, potentially eroding authentic self-expression.
  3. Highlights a regulatory gap: current EU AI Act and DSA may not cover indirect opinion steering via communication mediation, relevant for policy-oriented research.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Replicate the audit on other platforms (e.g., LinkedIn's 'Improve my post') to assess bias prevalence.
  • [ ] Develop countermeasures: e.g., debiasing prompts or transparent disclosure of AI edits to users.
  • [ ] Extend the opinion dynamics model to include user awareness or resistance to AI transformations.
  • [ ] Investigate whether similar amplification effects occur in other AI-mediated contexts (e.g., content moderation, recommendation systems).
