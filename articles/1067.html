1) Why-read

pArticleMap introduces a literature-mapping and agentic hypothesis-generation system that targets low-density bridge regions in nanomedicine, achieving a 61.0% future-neighborhood rate in retrospective benchmarks.

2) CRGP

C — Context

Nanomedicine research spans diverse subfields but remains fragmented, with AI applications mostly limited to property prediction and formulation optimization. Literature-based discovery (LBD) has traditionally relied on term co-occurrence or knowledge graphs, but lacks article-level frontier mapping and evidence-grounded hypothesis generation.

R — Related work

  • Swanson's LBD (Arrowsmith, BITOLA) using term co-occurrence and interactive ranking
  • SemMedDB and knowledge-graph-based hypothesis generation via subject-predicate-object relations
  • MOLIERE and AGATHA combining semantic graphs with learned rankers and historical holdout evaluation
  • iKraph and large-scale biomedical knowledge graphs for AI-powered discovery
  • Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) in scientific question answering

G — Gap

Existing AI in nanomedicine focuses on downstream optimization (property prediction, formulation) rather than upstream research direction selection. Prior LBD systems rely on concept co-occurrence or graph link prediction, not article-level frontier mapping with evidence-constrained LLM workflows, and lack rigorous retrospective evaluation with human assessment.

P — Proposal

pArticleMap combines article embeddings, similarity-graph analysis, sparse frontier extraction, structured evidence-pack retrieval, and an audited LLM agentic workflow to generate citation-grounded hypotheses targeting low-density bridge regions between literature clusters. The system is evaluated via a retrospective realization benchmark (generating later literature under a historical cutoff) and blinded human reader assessment.

3) Figures

Figure extraction is performed separately and inlined post-generation.

4) Experiments

Main results

Across 4 retrospective bundles, pArticleMap achieved a pooled gold recovery rate of 10.8%, recall@10 of 15.9%, and future-neighborhood rate of 61.0%. Human-agent agreement was modest, indicating internal scoring is useful but not a replacement for expert judgment.

Ablation

The future-neighborhood rate of 61.0% vs. exact paper recovery of 10.8% shows that the system reliably reaches the correct forward-looking neighborhood even when exact paper-level realization is difficult, highlighting the value of neighborhood-level evaluation over exact match.

Limitations

['Exact realization of later published papers remains deliberately difficult, with low gold recovery rates (10.8%).', 'Human-agent agreement is modest, so internal scoring cannot replace expert judgment.', 'Evaluation is limited to 4 retrospective bundles; generalizability to broader nanomedicine tasks is unconfirmed.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Provides a structured method to identify underexplored interfaces between nanomedicine subfields, directly supporting research direction selection.
  2. Demonstrates that LLM-based hypothesis generation can be grounded in evidence packs with provenance, addressing a key trust issue in AI for science.
  3. The 61.0% future-neighborhood rate suggests the system can guide researchers to promising areas even without exact prediction.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Apply pArticleMap to a broader set of nanomedicine domains and validate with prospective human studies.
  • [ ] Integrate the system into a researcher's daily workflow for real-time literature monitoring and hypothesis suggestion.
  • [ ] Explore combining pArticleMap with property prediction models to link hypothesis generation with formulation optimization.
  • [ ] Improve human-agent alignment by calibrating internal scoring with expert feedback loops.
