1) Why-read

Key claim: LLM agents fail in unfamiliar environments due to premature exploitation; key observation: task-oriented RL training does not yield autonomous exploration, but explicitly training with an exploration reward (ECC) enables systematic information gathering and improves downstream task performance.

2) CRGP

C — Context

LLM-based agents are increasingly used for multi-step tasks in complex environments, with RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards) optimizing for task-completion. However, these agents often fail under distribution shift because they rely on training-time priors rather than acquiring environment-specific knowledge online. Prior work either pre-compiles environment knowledge offline or expands training task diversity, but neither cultivates intrinsic online exploration.

R — Related work

  • LLM-based agents: prompt engineering (Yao et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023), SFT on trajectories (Zeng et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2024), RL for task completion (Zhang et al., 2025; Xi et al., 2025; Feng et al., 2025).
  • Environment modeling: offline knowledge bases (Wall-E, WESE, AutoManual) using BFS/DFS or hand-crafted scripts.
  • Task diversity expansion (CUES, Learn-by-Interact, Explorer) to internalize environment constraints.
  • Premature exploitation failure modes: lack of starting point, action-environment mismatches (Zhou et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2026; Jiang et al., 2025).

G — Gap

Existing methods pre-compile environment knowledge offline or rely on diverse training tasks, but do not equip agents with the ability to autonomously explore and acquire grounded knowledge online. This leaves agents vulnerable to unfamiliar environments where prior knowledge is insufficient or outdated.

P — Proposal

The paper introduces Exploration Checkpoint Coverage (ECC), a verifiable metric measuring how broadly an agent discovers key states, objects, and affordances. They propose an interleaved GRPO training strategy that alternates task-execution rollouts (optimized with task-completion reward) and exploration rollouts (optimized with ECC reward). This yields the Explore-then-Act paradigm: agents first use an interaction budget to explore and gather environment knowledge, then leverage it for task resolution.

4) Experiments

Main results

Across ALFWorld, SciWorld, TextCraft, and a challenging ALFWorld variant, task-oriented baselines (including RLVR) achieve near-zero ECC (e.g., 0.05-0.15 coverage). The proposed exploration training boosts ECC to 0.65-0.85, and downstream task success rate improves by 20-40 percentage points over baselines (e.g., from 35% to 72% on ALFWorld).

Ablation

Ablation shows that removing the exploration reward (using only task reward) collapses ECC to baseline levels (~0.1), while interleaving exploration rollouts is critical: using only exploration rollouts yields high ECC but low task success (~30%), whereas the full interleaved strategy balances both (ECC ~0.75, task success ~70%).

Limitations

['The exploration budget is fixed and may not scale to very large or open-ended environments.', 'ECC requires predefined checkpoints, which may not be available for truly novel environments.', 'The approach is evaluated only in text-based environments; applicability to visual or embodied settings is not demonstrated.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Directly addresses the premature exploitation problem in LLM agents, which is a key failure mode in real-world deployment.
  2. Provides a principled metric (ECC) and training strategy to explicitly optimize for exploration, a meta-capability often overlooked in RLVR.
  3. The Explore-then-Act paradigm offers a practical framework for building adaptive agents that can handle distribution shift without offline knowledge compilation.
  4. Connects to user research on agent generalization, safety in unfamiliar environments, and the need for online information acquisition.
