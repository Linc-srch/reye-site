1) Why-read

GenAI access raises average task performance but creates a new productivity divide driven by AI Interaction Competence (AIC), not traditional measures like GPA.

2) CRGP

C — Context

Prior work shows technology adoption requires complementary human capital, and skill-biased technical change often widens inequality. Emerging field experiments in customer service and consulting find heterogeneous GenAI gains, but lack controlled micro-evidence on mechanisms.

R — Related work

  • Bresnahan et al. (2002) on IT complementarities with human capital
  • Autor et al. (2003) on skill-biased technical change
  • Dell'Acqua et al. (2023) on GenAI productivity in consulting
  • Brynjolfsson et al. (2023) on GenAI in customer service
  • Noy & Zhang (2023) on writing productivity with LLMs

G — Gap

Existing studies document average GenAI gains but do not isolate the individual-level capability driving heterogeneous returns, nor test lightweight interventions to reduce variance. The construct of AI Interaction Competence (AIC) as a distinct form of human capital remains unexplored in controlled experiments.

P — Proposal

A randomized controlled experiment where early-career knowledge worker analogs self-study a technical domain with either traditional resources or LLM assistance. The key insight is that AIC—the ability to elicit, filter, and verify model outputs—predicts performance gains, and a conceptual map scaffolding intervention reduces outcome variance.

3) Figures

Figure extraction is performed separately and inlined post-generation.

4) Experiments

Main results

GenAI access increased mean task performance by 0.42 standard deviations (p<0.01). High-AIC participants (top quartile) gained +0.81 SD, while low-AIC participants (bottom quartile) showed -0.12 SD (non-significant). The scaffolding intervention reduced performance variance by 28% (p<0.05) without lowering mean.

Ablation

Removing the scaffolding intervention increased the coefficient of variation in performance by 34%, confirming that structured workflows mitigate inequality more than raw access alone.

Limitations

['Participants are student analogs, not experienced knowledge workers, limiting external validity.', 'The technical domain (self-study task) may not generalize to collaborative or creative workflows.', 'AIC is measured via a custom instrument; its psychometric properties need further validation.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Introduces AIC as a measurable human capital dimension critical for GenAI value capture, relevant to your work on human-AI interaction.
  2. Shows that simple process scaffolds (conceptual maps) can reduce inequality, informing your research on system design for equitable AI adoption.
  3. Highlights that traditional ability proxies (GPA) fail to predict GenAI performance, suggesting new hiring/training criteria for knowledge work.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Design micro-training interventions targeting AIC components (prompting, verification) and test their causal impact on productivity.
  • [ ] Explore whether similar scaffolding effects hold for other GenAI tools (e.g., code generation, data analysis) in your domain.
