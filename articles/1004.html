1) Why-read

LLM-driven cross-modal augmentation using written narratives to generate oral-style speech improves cognitive score prediction from small, imbalanced clinical datasets.

2) CRGP

C — Context

Spontaneous speech analysis is a promising non-invasive biomarker for early cognitive decline, but clinical datasets are small and class-imbalanced. Standard lexical augmentation risks altering clinically meaningful linguistic markers of dementia.

R — Related work

  • Binary dementia classification from speech using handcrafted features and traditional ML (Fraser et al., 2016; Tóth et al., 2015)
  • Deep learning with pretrained language models (e.g., BERT) for dementia detection (Balagopalan et al., 2020)
  • Multimodal fusion of acoustic and linguistic features (Hlédiková et al., 2022; Zolnour et al., 2025)
  • Text-based augmentation (synonym replacement, paraphrasing) for cognitive assessment (Igarashi and Nihei, 2022)
  • LLM-based synthetic clinical text generation (Hlédiková et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2025)

G — Gap

Prior work focuses on binary dementia classification, not continuous cognitive score regression. No existing study uses LLM-driven cross-modal augmentation (written-to-oral) for speech-based cognitive assessment, nor leverages written patient narratives as semantic anchors to generate clinically valid oral-style monologues.

P — Proposal

Propose an LLM-driven data augmentation framework that uses GPT-5 to generate oral-style monologues from written patient responses, preserving dementia-specific linguistic markers. Two selection strategies are compared: random class-balanced and similarity-guided class-balanced, where the latter selects synthetic samples semantically closest to real oral transcripts to improve consistency and reduce prediction error for minority low-score participants.

3) Figures

4) Experiments

Main results

On a Japanese corpus of 30 elderly participants (HDS scores), PLS regression on Sentence-BERT embeddings: similarity-guided augmentation reduced MAE for low-score (HDS<20) participants from 5.21 (baseline) to 3.84, while maintaining MAE for high-score participants (~2.5). Overall MAE improved from 3.12 (baseline) to 2.71.

Ablation

Random class-balanced augmentation yielded moderate but unstable improvements (MAE 2.89, std 0.45), while similarity-guided selection gave more consistent gains (MAE 2.71, std 0.21) and significantly reduced error for the minority low-score group.

Limitations

Small dataset (N=30) limits generalizability; GPT-5 generation may still introduce artifacts not fully representative of true spontaneous speech; reliance on written responses as anchors assumes availability of paired written-oral data, which may not exist in many clinical settings.

5) Why it matters

  1. Demonstrates a principled, clinically-guided data augmentation strategy that leverages cross-modal (written-to-oral) generation to address data scarcity and class imbalance in clinical speech analysis.
  2. Shows that similarity-guided filtering of synthetic samples is critical for maintaining clinical validity and improving regression performance, not just classification.
  3. Provides a template for using LLMs to generate task-specific synthetic data while preserving subtle linguistic biomarkers, relevant for other low-resource clinical NLP tasks.
  4. Highlights the potential of LLMs for data-efficient cognitive assessment, which could enable earlier and more scalable screening for dementia.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Validate on larger, multi-lingual clinical speech datasets (e.g., DementiaBank, ADReSS) to assess generalizability.
  • [ ] Explore alternative LLMs (e.g., GPT-4, open-source models) and compare generation quality and clinical fidelity.
  • [ ] Investigate integration of acoustic features (prosody, disfluency) alongside text embeddings for multimodal cognitive score prediction.
  • [ ] Design a human evaluation study to assess clinical plausibility of synthetic oral-style monologues by speech-language pathologists.
