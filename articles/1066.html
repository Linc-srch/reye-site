1) Why-read

Tabular foundation models (PFNs) outperform tuned GBMs and deep learning baselines in PD and LGD prediction, especially in small-data settings, without any hyperparameter tuning.

2) CRGP

C — Context

Credit risk models rely on accurate PD and LGD estimates for Basel/IFRS 9 compliance. Gradient boosting with SHAP is the current quasi-standard, but small-data portfolios (SME, low-default) remain challenging. Foundation models for tabular data have recently emerged, promising zero-shot transfer from synthetic pretraining.

R — Related work

  • Gradient boosting machines (GBMs) dominate tabular benchmarks (Lessmann et al., 2015; Gunnarsson et al., 2021)
  • Prior-Data Fitted Networks (PFNs) extend foundation model idea to tabular data via synthetic pretraining (Müller et al., 2021; Hollmann et al., 2025a)
  • Deep learning for tabular data (e.g., TabNet, NODE) has not consistently beaten GBMs (Shwartz-Ziv and Armon, 2022a)
  • Credit scoring benchmarking studies consolidate state-of-the-art for PD and LGD (Loterman et al., 2012; Baesens et al., 2003)

G — Gap

No systematic evaluation of tabular foundation models (TFMs) for credit risk prediction existed. Anecdotal evidence suggested PFNs work well on small datasets, but it was unclear if they could consistently match tuned baselines across diverse credit portfolios and targets (PD vs. LGD) under realistic conditions.

P — Proposal

The paper benchmarks four TFMs (PFNs) against GBMs, random forests, logistic regression, and advanced deep learning methods on 10 PD and 10 LGD datasets varying in size and imbalance. TFMs are used out-of-the-box (no tuning), while baselines are tuned via cross-validation. Key insight: TFMs achieve best average rank across all datasets and tasks, with largest gains on small datasets.

4) Experiments

Main results

Across 20 datasets, TFMs achieve best average AUC for PD (0.78 vs. 0.76 for XGBoost) and best average R² for LGD (0.32 vs. 0.28 for XGBoost). On the smallest quartile of datasets (N<5000), TFM AUC improves by +0.05 over XGBoost for PD; for LGD, R² improves by +0.08. Statistical significance confirmed via Wilcoxon signed-rank test (p<0.01).

Ablation

Ablation on dataset size: TFM advantage grows as dataset shrinks. For PD, TFM vs. XGBoost AUC gap is 0.01 for large datasets (>50k) but 0.05 for small datasets (<5k). For LGD, gap is 0.02 vs. 0.08 respectively. No hyperparameter tuning was performed for TFMs, confirming ease-of-use benefit.

Limitations

['TFMs are evaluated only on static cross-sectional data; temporal dynamics and concept drift are not addressed.', 'Only PD and LGD are considered; EAD and CCF modeling are omitted.', 'All TFMs are used zero-shot; fine-tuning on target data might further improve performance but was not tested.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Demonstrates that foundation models can disrupt tabular benchmarks in a high-stakes regulated domain, not just NLP/CV.
  2. Zero-shot inference eliminates costly hyperparameter tuning and retraining, which is operationally attractive for banks with many portfolios.
  3. Small-data performance is critical for SME lending, new products, and low-default portfolios—common pain points in practice.
  4. Cross-parameter consistency (same model for PD and LGD) could simplify model governance and regulatory validation.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Test fine-tuning of PFNs on target credit data to see if gains over zero-shot are meaningful.
  • [ ] Evaluate TFMs on time-series or panel data to assess robustness to economic cycles.
  • [ ] Benchmark TFMs on EAD and CCF modeling to confirm cross-parameter consistency.
  • [ ] Investigate fairness and bias properties of TFMs vs. GBMs using protected attributes.
