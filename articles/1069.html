1) Why-read

Faculty AI adoption is driven not by attitudes or institutional support but by a coherent 'AI pedagogical orientation' reflecting beliefs about AI's role in disciplinary thinking and expertise.

2) CRGP

C — Context

LLMs are widely available in higher education but instructor adoption varies greatly. Prior work has focused on attitudes, perceived usefulness, and barriers, yet fails to explain why instructors respond differently to similar affordances. This study targets pedagogically motivated STEM faculty (RCSA Cottrell awardees) to uncover deeper mechanisms.

R — Related work

  • Survey-based studies on instructor AI adoption (e.g., motivation, perceived usefulness, technical barriers) [53]
  • Qualitative work on instructors' multiple interpretations of AI (tool, knowledge source, threat) [47]
  • Studies on LLM performance in physics/chemistry problems, lab reports, and instructional materials [50, 42, 54, 1, 10, 29, 45]
  • Research on computation integration in physics education (PICUP community) [2, 8, 9, 58]
  • Technology-adoption models (e.g., TAM) used as interpretive frameworks

G — Gap

Existing technology-adoption models emphasize access, perceived usefulness, and attitudes, but they do not explain variation in adoption when AI directly interacts with disciplinary reasoning and knowledge production. The study identifies a missing construct—AI pedagogical orientation—that captures faculty beliefs about AI's role in learning and expertise.

P — Proposal

The authors conducted a mixed-methods survey of 90 STEM faculty, using exploratory factor analysis to identify 'AI pedagogical orientation' as a coherent construct. This orientation strongly predicts self-reported AI use across research, teaching, and professional activities, and reflects differing views on AI's role in disciplinary thinking rather than simple positive/negative attitudes.

3) Figures

Figure extraction is performed separately and inlined post-generation.

4) Experiments

Main results

The survey of 90 RCSA Cottrell faculty found that AI pedagogical orientation strongly predicted self-reported AI use (p < 0.001, effect size not reported in abstract). Institutional initiatives, demographics, and information sources showed comparatively weak associations. Qualitative analysis confirmed the construct reflects views on disciplinary thinking, learning, and expertise development.

Ablation

The study did not report explicit ablation experiments; however, the factor analysis showed that AI pedagogical orientation was a stronger predictor than institutional support, demographics, or information sources, indicating its dominant role over other variables.

Limitations

['Sample is limited to 90 pedagogically motivated STEM faculty (RCSA Cottrell awardees), not representative of all instructors.', 'Self-reported AI use may not align with actual behavior; no observational or log data.', 'Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality between orientation and adoption.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Suggests that faculty development for AI should focus on disciplinary beliefs and pedagogical reasoning, not just tool training.
  2. Connects to your interest in how epistemic beliefs shape technology adoption in education.
  3. Highlights that AI adoption may follow a different pathway than prior technologies (e.g., computation) due to its direct interaction with knowledge production.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Design interventions that help faculty reflect on and articulate their AI pedagogical orientation.
  • [ ] Conduct longitudinal studies to see if orientation changes with experience or training.
  • [ ] Develop survey instruments to measure AI pedagogical orientation in broader faculty populations.
  • [ ] Compare AI pedagogical orientation with similar constructs in computation integration (e.g., PICUP).
