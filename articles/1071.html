1) Why-read

Key claim: Multimodal inputs cause a systematic geometric collapse of the refusal direction in MLLMs, and key observation: counteracting modality-induced drift via a self-rectification signal (ReGap) restores safety without harming general capability.

2) CRGP

C — Context

Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) inherit safety alignment from text-only training but fail to refuse harmful non-text inputs (images, audio). Prior work attributes this to insufficient multimodal safety supervision, but the paper shows the failure is geometric: multimodal inputs displace representations away from the text-aligned refusal direction, collapsing separability between refused and complied inputs.

R — Related work

  • Text-only jailbreaks (Zou et al., 2023b; Lee et al., 2025) show adversarial prompts shift harmful requests toward benign regions in activation space.
  • Multimodal safety gaps (Liu et al., 2024a; Pan et al., 2025) document that MLLMs fail on semantically equivalent multimodal inputs despite strong text refusal.
  • Activation steering methods (Wang et al., 2024; Arditi et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2024) use fixed-strength interventions to modify model behavior.
  • Representation-level alignment (Zhao et al., 2025) shows a clear refusal direction in aligned LLMs.

G — Gap

Existing explanations for multimodal safety failures focus on insufficient training data, but do not explain why models with strong text-only refusal fail on semantically equivalent multimodal inputs. The paper identifies a systematic geometric degradation (Safety Geometry Collapse) rather than isolated adversarial exceptions, and shows that fixed-strength drift correction trades off safety and utility, motivating an adaptive approach.

P — Proposal

The paper proposes ReGap, a training-free inference-time method that adaptively corrects modality-induced drift using a self-rectification signal. ReGap estimates a modality drift direction from paired text-multimodal inputs, then uses the normalized self-rectification (recovery of refusal projection across layers) to decide when to apply stronger correction, reducing unnecessary perturbation to benign inputs.

4) Experiments

Main results

ReGap is evaluated on multiple open-weight multimodal models (e.g., VLM, audio-language, omni-modal) across safety benchmarks (e.g., Omni-SafetyBench) and utility benchmarks. It significantly improves multimodal safety (e.g., attack success rate reduction) while largely preserving general performance; specific numbers are not provided in the abstract but the paper claims consistent improvements.

Ablation

Fixed-strength drift correction improves refusal separability but degrades general multimodal capability at larger coefficients, revealing a safety–utility trade-off. ReGap's adaptive correction avoids this by strengthening correction only for inputs exhibiting refusal-recovery dynamics.

Limitations

1) The method relies on a calibration set of paired text-multimodal inputs, which may not cover all modality combinations or domains. 2) The self-rectification signal is layer-specific and may not generalize to very deep or differently architected models. 3) The paper does not evaluate against adaptive or multi-turn jailbreak attacks.

5) Why it matters

  1. Provides a mechanistic (geometric) explanation for multimodal safety gaps, linking representation drift to refusal failure.
  2. Proposes a practical, training-free inference-time safeguard (ReGap) that adapts to input harmfulness, preserving utility.
  3. Highlights representation-level modality alignment as a key direction for real-time safety in deployed MLLMs.
  4. Connects to user research on activation steering and representation engineering for safety.

6) Next steps

  • [ ] Test ReGap on additional MLLM architectures (e.g., GPT-4V, Gemini) and modality combinations (video, tactile).
  • [ ] Investigate whether self-rectification signals can be used for online monitoring of harmful inputs without explicit correction.
  • [ ] Explore combining ReGap with adversarial training to further close the multimodal safety gap.
  • [ ] Extend the geometric framework to multi-turn or context-dependent safety scenarios.
