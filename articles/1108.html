1) Why-read

Federated Martingale Posterior (FMP) replaces the prior-likelihood pair with a predictive distribution and enables one-shot embarrassingly parallel Bayesian inference by having clients upload compressed data embeddings instead of raw data.

2) CRGP

C — Context

Federated Bayesian neural networks require specifying a prior and likelihood, which is difficult for overparameterized models and sensitive to misspecification. The martingale posterior (predictive Bayes) avoids this by using a predictive distribution and refitting on predictive samples, but a direct federated implementation would require sharing local datasets.

R — Related work

  • Martingale posterior (Fong et al., 2023) and predictive Bayes (Bissiri et al., 2016) replace prior-likelihood with predictive distribution.
  • Set Transformer with induced self-attention blocks (ISAB) (Lee et al., 2019) for exchangeable predictive distributions.
  • Consensus Monte Carlo (Scott et al., 2016) as a baseline for federated Bayesian inference.
  • Federated Bayesian learning (Al-Shedivat et al., 2021; Zecchin et al., 2024).

G — Gap

Existing federated Bayesian methods require specifying a prior and likelihood, which is challenging for modern overparameterized models. A naive distributed martingale posterior would require clients to share entire local datasets, violating privacy and communication constraints.

P — Proposal

FMP proposes a one-shot protocol where each client compresses its local dataset into a small set of trainable embeddings via an attention-based pooling block, uploads only these embeddings, and the server aggregates them to run the centralized predictive sampler. A meta-training procedure aligns FMP samples with centralized MP samples across related tasks.

3) Figures

4) Experiments

Main results

On MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 under both homogeneous and heterogeneous client partitions, FMP closely matches centralized MP in accuracy and expected calibration error (ECE), and significantly improves calibration over consensus-style baselines (CFMP). For example, on CIFAR-10 with 10 clients, FMP achieves ECE of 0.032 vs CFMP 0.089 and centralized MP 0.028.

Ablation

The number of compressed embeddings per client (K) is ablated: using K=10 on CIFAR-10 yields ECE 0.032, while K=5 gives 0.041 and K=20 gives 0.030, showing diminishing returns beyond K=10.

Limitations

['The meta-training procedure requires a corpus of related tasks, which may not always be available.', 'The method assumes clients have datasets of equal size and the same predictive model architecture.', 'The compression via trainable embeddings may lose information compared to sharing raw data, potentially affecting performance on more complex datasets.']

5) Why it matters

  1. Provides a practical way to perform Bayesian inference in federated settings without specifying priors, relevant for uncertainty quantification in distributed systems.
  2. The one-shot communication protocol is highly efficient and privacy-preserving, aligning with constraints in real-world federated learning.
  3. The use of set transformers and meta-learning to align distributions offers a template for other federated Bayesian methods.

6) Next steps
