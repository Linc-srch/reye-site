Research Direction

研究方向

Domain: Operating System & Kernel Security, Software & Memory Safety, Vulnerability Research & Exploitation, Malware & Software Supply-Chain Security. Core Research Objective: Tracking research, disclosed vulnerabilities, and engineering advances in the security of the underlying computing stack — operating systems, kernels, runtimes, compilers, and the software supply chain — independent of any single vendor ecosystem. The focus is on how systems are broken (exploitation, memory-corruption, privilege escalation, sandbox/container escape) and how they are hardened (mitigations, isolation, formal/automated verification, secure-by-design). Taxonomy of Research Areas: 1. OS & Kernel Security: kernel exploitation and hardening, privilege escalation, syscall/driver attack surface, secure boot, trusted execution environments (TEE/SGX/TrustZone), hypervisor and virtualization security. 2. Memory & Software Safety: memory-corruption bugs (buffer/heap overflow, use-after-free), mitigations (ASLR, CFI, stack canaries), memory-safe languages (Rust), fuzzing and sanitizers, static/dynamic analysis. 3. Vulnerability Research & Exploitation: CVE analysis, n-day/0-day exploitation techniques, ROP/JOP, patch diffing, responsible disclosure, exploit mitigation bypasses. 4. Malware & Reverse Engineering: malware families and campaigns, binary/firmware analysis, anti-analysis/evasion, transparency into attacker tooling. 5. Software Supply-Chain Security: compromised packages/dependencies (npm/PyPI), build-system and CI/CD attacks, code-signing, SBOM, dependency integrity. 6. Isolation & Containment: containers and sandboxes, microkernels, capability systems, confinement and least-privilege architectures. Scope boundary: This domain covers the security of general computing systems and software. It is distinct from 'SOC' (security operations, detection, threat intelligence), 'Harmony Security' (HarmonyOS-specific endpoint/ecosystem benchmarking), 'Cryptography' (cryptographic primitives/protocols), and 'AI Security' (alignment and ML-model safety). Pure machine-learning papers with no systems-security angle are out of scope.

MFA Prompt Bombing: Why Your Second Factor Isn't Saving You

MFA Prompt Bombing: Why Your Second Factor Isn't Saving You

MFA prompt bombing exploits push-based multi-factor authentication by repeatedly sending approval requests to a user's device until they accept, often combined with vishing calls impersonating IT support. The 2022 Cisco breach demonstrated its effectiveness against mature security programs. Attackers need only valid credentials and a push-MFA-enabled portal. Once approved, the login appears legitimate, bypassing security alerts. Organizations can mitigate this by using phishing-resistant MFA factors like FIDO2 keys, blocking compromised passwords at the source, and adding risk-based conditional access policies. Push MFA alone does not eliminate risk due to lack of context in approval prompts.

MFA Prompt Bombing 攻击利用基于推送的多因素认证，通过反复向用户设备发送批准请求，直到用户接受，常结合冒充IT支持的语音钓鱼电话。2022年的Cisco入侵事件展示了该技术对成熟安全程序的有效性。攻击者只需有效凭证和启用推送MFA的门户。一旦批准，登录看似合法，绕过安全警报。组织可通过使用抗钓鱼的MFA因素（如FIDO2密钥）、从源头阻止泄露密码以及添加基于风险的条件访问策略来缓解。仅靠推送MFA无法消除风险，因为批准提示缺乏上下文。
